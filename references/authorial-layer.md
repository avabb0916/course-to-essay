# The authorial layer (making it original, not a repackage)

The difference between a good summary and a real essay is **whose mind it came
through**. A summary relays what the course said. An essay is the author
thinking, using the course as one input among their own experience and views.

This file is about deliberately adding the author's layer so the published piece
reads as original work — because the user publishes it as their own thinking,
with no attribution to the source course.

## Why this matters

A reader (or the course's creator) can tell when writing is just reworded
slides. The tells:

- the essay walks the course's sections **in the same order**;
- it reproduces a named framework with the **same labels and grouping**;
- it reuses the instructor's **signature lines** almost verbatim;
- it has **no point of view** — nothing the author argues, only relays;
- it contains **nothing the course didn't** — no outside connection, no example
  from the author's own life, no pushback.

Fix all five and the piece becomes genuinely the author's.

## The four moves

### 1. Take a position (agree-and-extend, or push back)
Decide what YOU think. You don't have to disagree — but you must do more than
relay. Two reliable patterns:

- **Agree-and-extend:** "The course is right that X. What it doesn't say is that
  this also means Y." You carry the idea one step further than the source.
- **Reframe / gentle pushback:** "It's framed as a problem of A. I think A is
  just the visible symptom; the real thing is B." Reasoned, not contrarian.

One genuine position is enough to give a whole essay a spine.

### 2. Find your own organizing angle
The course has an architecture (its section order, its model). Your essay needs
a *different* one — a single idea you re-derive everything from. Then the
material reorganizes itself around your idea instead of the syllabus.

> Worked example. A course presented three learning pain points (怕 / 远 / 乱)
> as a flat list, then three learning stages. Derivative version: three sections
> named 怕/远/乱. **Authorial version:** organize the whole essay around one
> claim — "you're not bad at this, you're missing a ladder" — and let the three
> pains appear *as consequences* of not having a ladder. Same raw material,
> different mind running it.

### 3. Add first-person texture (honestly)
Lived observation is the strongest originality signal and the hardest to fake-
detect because it's genuinely yours. Use:

- **Observations** you can stand behind generically: "I've watched capable
  people freeze at exactly the moment they should start." (No invented facts.)
- **Placeholders for specific personal facts.** Where a concrete anecdote,
  number, or named project would land best, insert
  `【可替换为你的真实经历：例如你自己第一次上手某工具的具体故事】`
  and let the user fill it. This is the ONLY honest way to get specific personal
  detail you don't have.

**Never** invent checkable specifics (a fake company, a made-up statistic, a
fabricated "a student of mine named…") and present them as the author's own.
Fabrication is both dishonest and the fastest way to get caught — it's worse
than no anecdote.

### 4. Add what the course didn't
Bring in something from outside the deck:

- a connection to another field ("this is the same dynamic as compounding
  interest / language learning / weight training");
- a counter-example or boundary case the course skipped;
- a sharper metaphor than the one the course used;
- "here's the part nobody mentions" — the unglamorous caveat.

This is the clearest evidence you metabolized the idea instead of moving it.

## Handling the course's frameworks and lines

- **Frameworks:** understand them, then rebuild. Rename the parts, change the
  grouping, or re-derive them from your angle. The reader should get the
  *insight*, not recognize *the slide*.
- **Signature lines:** the instructor's memorable phrasings are their most
  recognizable fingerprint. Transform them into your own voice, or write a
  better line. Don't quote them.
- **Course-specific cases (named people/companies):** these are traceable to the
  source. Prefer to generalize ("someone with no coding background shipped a
  real project") or replace with the user's own via a placeholder, rather than
  reproducing the course's specific roster.

## Using a personal experience document

When the user provides a resume, career timeline, or biographical text, the
authorial layer gets a concrete upgrade: placeholders become real anecdotes.
This is the single highest-impact thing you can do to make the essay feel
genuinely personal. The workflow:

1. **At Phase 2.5 (before writing):** scan the document and note which life
   episodes match each placeholder theme. You don't need to match precisely
   yet — just flag "the story about X would work in the section on Y." This
   lets you write the surrounding prose with the real anecdote's shape in mind,
   so it fits rather than being pasted in afterward.

2. **At Phase 3.5 (after writing):** fill each `【可替换…】` slot with the
   matched episode. Choose episodes that have a **clear decision moment** and a
   **visible consequence** — these make the strongest stories.

3. **Identity obscuring (mandatory):**
   - Company names → "某 SaaS 公司" / "一家行业头部公司" (never the real name)
   - Named colleagues / clients → "一位前辈" / "当时的合伙人"
   - Specific numbers (salary, revenue) → vague ranges or omit
   - Dates → "几年前" / omit entirely
   - The rule: a reader who doesn't know the author cannot identify the
     specific organization or people involved.

4. **Keep the emotional truth.** The feelings, the decision stakes, the lesson
   learned — these are what make an anecdote real. Strip identity, keep heart.

5. After filling, confirm to the user which episode you used and what you
   obscured, so they can verify before publishing.

**Never** pull details from the experience document unprompted. It is private
input — only draw from it for explicit placeholder filling or when the user
says "充实真实经历".

## The derivative check (run before delivering)

Skim the draft next to your course notes. For each paragraph ask: *could the
course's creator point at this and say "that's just my slide, reworded"?*

- If yes for a framework paragraph → rebuild it from your angle.
- If yes for a memorable line → rewrite it in your voice.
- If the whole section order mirrors the course → reorganize around your idea.
- If you can't find a single place where the author has a view → add one.

Pass this check and the essay stands on its own.
