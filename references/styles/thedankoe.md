# Voice: Dan Koe (thedankoe)

Dan Koe writes long-form essays that feel like a sharp friend reorganizing your
worldview. The reader should finish feeling like they *thought their way* to a
new conclusion, not like they were lectured. Slow content that forces both
writer and reader to do the ordering work — the opposite of pre-digested
listicles.

## The structure: APAG

Dan's backbone is **APAG**. Use it as the spine; the section count is flexible.

- **A — Attention.** Open by agitating a relatable problem and painting a
  picture, ideally through a lived experience. Short punchy lines. Make the
  reader feel *seen* before you make any argument. (Classic Koe move: a rapid
  list of mundane painful moments — "8 hours of unfulfilling work. Sit in
  traffic. Argue. Watch TV. Repeat.")
- **P — Perspective.** Name a mainstream belief you think is *wrong*, and say
  why. This is the pivot of the whole essay. The course almost always hands you
  this for free (the assumption it sets out to correct).
- **A — Advantage.** Show what opens up once the reader adopts the new
  perspective. The frameworks, the reframes, the "here's why this changes
  everything." This is the meat — the course's models and cases live here.
- **G — Gamify.** Give concrete, do-able steps and a challenge. Turn insight
  into action the reader can start today. End with a line they'll remember.

## Sentence-level habits

- **Second person.** Talk to "you." Direct, personal, a little provocative.
- **Rhythm through length variation.** Long flowing sentence, then a three-word
  punch. Single sentences as their own paragraphs for emphasis. White space is
  a tool.
- **Rule of 3.** Three main points, often three sub-points each. Flexible, but
  triads feel intentional and land well.
- **Storytelling ≠ a story.** Anecdotes, research, examples, metaphors, numbers
  — anything that helps the reader *understand*. The講故事的爸爸 case, the
  500万→5万 number: these are storytelling.
- **Plain language over jargon.** Introduce a term, then immediately make it
  concrete. Never leave the reader holding an undefined abstraction.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Short paragraphs. Generous breaks.
- **Bold sparingly** for the lines that carry weight — the reframes and
  payoffs, not random emphasis.

## Do

- Earn attention in the first 3 lines with a problem the reader lives.
- Make the "wrong mainstream view" explicit and then dismantle it.
- **Organize around YOUR angle** (from Phase 2.5), not the course's section
  order. Rebuild any framework in your own words and grouping — the reader
  should get the insight, not recognize the source's slide.
- **Carry a real opinion.** Koe always has a take. Agree-and-extend or push back
  somewhere; relaying isn't enough.
- Land each section on a quotable line **you wrote** — not the instructor's.
- Close with a single, screenshot-worthy sentence of your own.

## Don't

- Don't write a summary or a thread. No "Point 1 / Point 2" scaffolding, no
  "1/12" numbering.
- Don't open with a definition or "In this article we will…".
- Don't keep classroom artifacts (interaction prompts, "扫码", quiz questions,
  "今天讲N个话题").
- Don't reproduce a named model's exact labels/order, or lift the instructor's
  signature lines verbatim — that's the derivative fingerprint to avoid.
- Don't fabricate specific personal facts; use `【可替换…】` placeholders.
- Don't over-hedge. Koe is confident. State the perspective plainly.
- Don't bury the thesis. The reader should know what you believe early.

## Worked mini-example (shape only)

> **Hook (A):** a rapid-fire picture of the reader's frustrating status quo →
> "And then what? The tools changed. You didn't."
>
> **Perspective (P):** "You think you understand X. But your understanding is
> stuck in [old era]." Name the comfortable-but-wrong belief; call it out.
>
> **Advantage (A):** "Here's what actually changed." Walk the course's
> framework(s) in plain language, each section ending on a punchline; weave in
> the real cases and numbers as proof.
>
> **Gamify (G):** "Here's how to start." The course's action steps as a do-able
> sequence + a challenge → one memorable closing line.

The validated essay (AI 数据 / "所有短期红利都是假的") follows exactly this
shape — use it as the north star for tone and pacing.
